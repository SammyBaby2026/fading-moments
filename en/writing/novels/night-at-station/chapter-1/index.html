The Night at the Station
Chapter 1

The Delay

The train was delayed. Not unusual for a winter evening in the mountains, but enough to make him wait longer than expected. He stood on the platform, watching his breath turn to mist in the cold air, thinking about how some waits feel longer than others.

The station was small—a single platform, a wooden shelter with peeling paint, and a clock that clicked rather than ticked. Somewhere in the distance, a dog barked. Somewhere closer, the wind found a gap in the roof and whistled through.

He thought about the letter in his pocket. The one he hadn't sent. The one he kept rewriting in his mind, each version more honest than the last, each version more impossible to deliver.

Memory is a strange thing. It keeps what we want to forget and fades what we try to hold onto.

The station master appeared briefly, checked his watch against the broken clock, and disappeared again into his warm office. The man on the platform envied him that warmth—that certainty of purpose, that small domain where everything had its place.

He remembered her saying once that train stations were liminal spaces—neither here nor there, moments suspended between departures and arrivals. He hadn't understood then. He thought he did now.

The fog was settling in, softening the edges of the world. Soon, he knew, the mountains would disappear completely, and the station would become an island in a sea of gray. He found something comforting in that—a small, lit space surrounded by nothing, holding its ground against the void.
