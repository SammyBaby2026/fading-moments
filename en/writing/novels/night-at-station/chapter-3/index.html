The Night at the Station
Chapter 3

The Station Master

The station master had worked there for forty years. He had seen thousands of travelers pass through, each carrying their own stories. Some were easy to read—the businessmen with their briefcases and impatience, the lovers with their lingering goodbyes, the families with their chaos and laughter.

But the man on the platform tonight was different. He wasn't waiting for a train, the station master realized. He was waiting for something else entirely. Something that might never arrive.

The station master brought him a cup of tea. It was something his wife always did when they had visitors—offer warmth to strangers in the cold. The man took it with surprise, as if kindness were unexpected.

"Delayed again," the station master said, not as a complaint but as an observation. "The mountain weather. Unpredictable."

The man nodded. "Do you ever get used to it? The waiting?"

The station master considered this. "I used to mind it. When I was young, I thought every delay was time stolen from me. Now I think maybe time isn't something you own. It's something that passes through you, like these trains."

They stood in silence, watching the snow fall. The station master had learned that some conversations don't need many words. That sometimes the most important thing is simply being there, sharing the same space, breathing the same cold air.
