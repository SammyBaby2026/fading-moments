The Night at the Station
Chapter 2

The Letter

The letter had been in his pocket for three weeks. He had written it on a Tuesday, the day after she left. The paper was soft now from being folded and unfolded, read and reread, until the creases were permanent scars.

He took it out sometimes, not to read it—he knew every word by heart—but to feel its weight. Four paragraphs. Two hundred and seventeen words. The closest he had ever come to telling her the truth.

The snow had started again, small flakes drifting down like questions he couldn't answer. He watched them land on the platform and disappear, and he thought about how everything beautiful seems to vanish if you watch it long enough.

In the letter, he had written about the station. About how it felt like the edge of the world. About how waiting for a train is a kind of faith—the belief that something will arrive even when you can't see it coming.

He never mailed it. Every morning, he would walk to the post office, stand in line, and then leave. Some truths are easier to write than to send. Some feelings are safer when they remain unsaid.

Now, standing in the cold, he wondered if she would have understood. Or if she already knew. Perhaps that was why she had left—not because he said too much, but because he never said enough.
