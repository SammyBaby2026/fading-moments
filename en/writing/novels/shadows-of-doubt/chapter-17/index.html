Shadows of Doubt
Chapter 17: Brother Against Brother
March 21, 2026
4,500 words · Chapter 17 of 20

The Harrison Steel Mill had been a tomb for fifteen years, and now Jack walked through its gates knowing it might become his own. The night air carried the smell of rust and decay, of metal long abandoned to the elements. Above him, the skeletal remains of the blast furnace loomed against the moonlit sky—a rusted giant that had once breathed fire and now only whispered ghosts.

"You didn't have to come," Jack said, not looking back.

Sarah's footsteps crunched on the gravel behind him. "Yes, I did. He's my brother."

"Half-brother. And he's tried to kill us both."

"That doesn't change what he is." Sarah stepped up beside him, her face pale in the moonlight. "Or what I need to say to him."

Jack wanted to argue, to tell her that Lucas was beyond reason, beyond redemption—that the man who called himself The Machinist had crossed a line from which there was no return. But looking at Sarah's determined expression, he knew it would be wasted breath. She had made up her mind, and Jack knew better than to try to change it.

They had received the message three hours ago, shortly after that cryptic text about a new Machinist taking up the mantle. An address. Nothing more. But Jack had recognized it immediately—the site of the original explosion, the graveyard of twenty-three workers, the birthplace of all their nightmares.

"He's waiting for us," Jack said, his hand resting on the gun holstered beneath his jacket. "This is a trap."

"I know."

"He'll try to kill us."

"I know that too." Sarah turned to face him, her eyes dark and serious. "But I also know that somewhere inside that monster is a boy who was thrown away like garbage. A child who never had a chance. I need to see if that boy still exists, Jack. I need to know if there's anything left to save."

Jack nodded slowly. "Then let's find out."

***

The interior of the mill was worse than Jack remembered. Fifteen years of abandonment had left the structure dangerously unstable—floors sagging, walls cracked, the air thick with dust and the smell of oxidized metal. They moved carefully, flashlights cutting through the darkness, each step echoing in the vast empty space.

"Lucas!" Sarah's voice rang out, clear and unafraid. "I know you're here! Show yourself!"

For a long moment, nothing. Then, from somewhere in the depths of the building, they heard it—the distinctive click of a hammer being drawn back on a revolver.

"Welcome home, sister."

The voice came from everywhere and nowhere, amplified by the acoustics of the ruined mill. Jack spun, trying to locate the source, his hand tightening on his weapon.

"I'm not here to fight you," Sarah called out. "I'm here to talk."

"Talk?" A laugh, harsh and hollow. "That's rich, coming from the woman who shot our dear brother Daniel."

Sarah flinched. "Daniel tried to kill Jack. He gave me no choice."

"There's always a choice, Sarah. You just made the wrong one."

A figure emerged from the shadows on the upper catwalk—a silhouette against the broken windows, backlit by the moon. Lucas Chen, formerly Lucas Harrison before his identity had been stripped away along with his childhood. The new Machinist.

"You look like him," Sarah said softly. "Like Daniel. Like... like me."

"We share the same poisoned blood," Lucas agreed. "The same cursed genes. The same father who threw me away like yesterday's trash." He began to descend the metal stairs, each step deliberate and controlled. "But Daniel was weak. He let his sentimentality cloud his judgment. He actually thought you might understand, might join us. I know better."

"Join you in what?" Jack spoke for the first time, his voice carrying across the empty floor. "Killing more innocent people? Destroying more lives?"

"Innocent?" Lucas's laugh was genuine this time, full of bitter humor. "There are no innocent people in this world, Detective Morrison. Only the guilty and the soon-to-be-guilty. The powerful and the powerless. The users and the used." He reached the bottom of the stairs and stepped into the light of their flashlights, and Sarah gasped.

Lucas was shirtless, and his body was a roadmap of suffering. Scars crisscrossed his torso—some thin and surgical, others thick and ragged, the marks of belts and burns and things Jack didn't want to imagine. On his left shoulder, a crude tattoo depicted a broken gear, the number 15 carved into its center.

"My foster father was a religious man," Lucas said, noting their expressions. "He believed that spare the rod, spoil the child. Of course, in his case, it wasn't just rods. It was belts, coat hangers, cigarettes, whatever was handy." He turned slowly, letting them see the full extent of the damage. "The state paid him three hundred dollars a month to take care of me. I cost him a lot more than that in medical bills, I'm sure."

"Lucas..." Sarah's voice broke. "I'm so sorry. I didn't know."

"How could you?" He faced her, and for the first time, Jack saw something other than hatred in his eyes—pain, raw and ancient, the wound of a child who had never understood why he wasn't wanted. "You were the lucky one, Sarah. The chosen one. You got the warm house, the loving parents, the future. I got the basement, the beatings, and the knowledge that I was worthless. That I deserved every blow because I was garbage. Unwanted. Unloved."

"You didn't deserve that," Sarah said, stepping forward. "No child deserves that."

"But I got it anyway." Lucas's voice hardened. "Every day for sixteen years, until I was old enough and strong enough to leave. Until I was big enough to hit back." He flexed his hands, and Jack noticed the scarring on his knuckles. "My foster father died in a house fire two weeks after I left. Terrible tragedy. The investigators never found the accelerant I'd used."

"You killed him."

"I freed myself." Lucas's eyes gleamed in the darkness. "Just like I'm going to free everyone else who's trapped in this corrupt system. The workers who died in this mill—they were killed by greed, by men like our father who valued profit over people. The men I've targeted since then—each one deserved what they got. Each one had blood on their hands."

"And Dr. Harrison?" Jack asked. "Your own half-brother?"

"A Harrison." The word came out like a curse. "Born with a silver spoon in his mouth, raised in luxury, handed everything he ever wanted. And what did he do with his privilege? He blackmailed our father. He tried to profit from the pain of others. He was no better than the rest of them."

"So you became judge, jury, and executioner," Jack said. "You decided who lived and who died."

"Someone had to." Lucas spread his arms. "The system doesn't work, Detective. You know that better than anyone. You've spent your career watching guilty men walk free, watching the rich buy their way out of consequences. I simply decided to do something about it."

"By becoming a monster."

"By becoming effective." Lucas took a step toward Sarah. "Daniel understood that, at least in the beginning. He understood that words and investigations and court cases don't stop evil men. Only fear stops them. The fear that they might be next."

"Daniel died because he couldn't let go of his hatred," Sarah said. "Don't make the same mistake, Lucas. Please."

"Let go?" Lucas's voice rose, echoing off the metal walls. "How can I let go of something that's carved into my very flesh?" He gestured at his scarred body. "Every mark is a reminder. Every scar is a promise. I am what they made me, Sarah. A weapon. A monster. A ghost of vengeance."

"You're my brother," Sarah said firmly. "And I won't give up on you."

For a moment, something flickered in Lucas's eyes—uncertainty, perhaps, or a desperate longing for connection. But then his face hardened again, the mask of The Machinist sliding back into place.

"Sentimental fool," he spat. "Just like Daniel. You'll die just like him too."

He moved faster than Jack expected, a blur of motion in the darkness. A gun appeared in his hand—not a revolver this time, but a sleek semi-automatic—and he fired three times in rapid succession. Jack threw himself sideways, tackling Sarah to the ground as bullets sparked off the metal floor where they'd been standing.

"Stay down!" Jack rolled to his feet, drawing his own weapon. "Lucas! Stop this!"

"Make me!"

The fight that followed was brutal and intimate, a dance of violence in the ruins of the mill. Lucas was a skilled combatant—better than Daniel had been, more controlled, more ruthless. He moved with the efficiency of someone who had learned to fight not for sport but for survival, every strike calculated to disable or kill.

Jack gave ground, dodging and weaving, trying to find an opening. He couldn't shoot—not with Sarah so close, not when part of him still hoped to take Lucas alive. But Lucas had no such compunctions. He pressed the attack, forcing Jack back toward a gaping hole in the floor where machinery had once stood.

"You're good, Detective," Lucas acknowledged, breathing hard. "Better than I expected. But you're still fighting by rules. Still trying to arrest me, to save me. That's your weakness."

"It's what makes me different from you," Jack gasped, blocking a kick that would have broken his ribs. "I'm not a murderer."

"No. You're just a failure. A disgraced cop who couldn't even protect his own partner."

The words hit harder than any physical blow. Jack stumbled, and Lucas pressed his advantage, driving a knee into Jack's stomach and following with an elbow strike that sent him sprawling.

"Jack!" Sarah screamed.

Lucas stood over Jack, his gun aimed at the detective's head. "I told Daniel we should have killed you from the start. You're too persistent. Too stubborn. Too damn moral for your own good."

"Then do it," Jack wheezed. "Pull the trigger. Prove you're the monster you think you are."

Lucas hesitated, his finger tightening on the trigger. For a moment, Jack saw the war in his eyes—the conflict between the man he might have been and the weapon he had become.

"Lucas, don't!" Sarah was on her feet, moving toward them. "He's not the enemy! The real enemy is the corruption, the system, the men who hurt you! Not Jack!"

"The system is everywhere," Lucas said without looking at her. "In the police that looked the other way when I was being beaten. In the judges that returned me to my foster father again and again. In the reporters who write about justice but never deliver it." His voice dropped to a whisper. "He's part of it, Sarah. They all are."

"Then kill me too," Sarah said, stepping between them. "If you're going to kill him, you'll have to kill me first."

Lucas stared at her, his gun wavering. "You'd die for him? A man you've known for a few weeks?"

"I'd die for what's right," Sarah said. "Just like you think you're doing. But this isn't right, Lucas. Killing Jack won't change what happened to you. It won't heal your scars. It won't bring back your childhood."

"Nothing will bring that back."

"No. But you can still have a future. A life. A chance to be something other than what they made you."

Lucas laughed, but it sounded broken. "You sound like Daniel. He believed in redemption too. Look where it got him."

"Daniel died trying to kill the wrong person," Sarah said gently. "You're smarter than that, Lucas. I know you are. Deep down, beneath all the anger and the pain, you're still that little boy who just wanted to be loved. Who just wanted a family."

The gun trembled in Lucas's hand. "Stop."

"I'm your family, Lucas. I'm your sister. And I'm not going to abandon you like everyone else did."

"I said stop!"

But Sarah didn't stop. She kept moving forward, step by step, until she was close enough to touch him. Until she could reach out and place her hand over his on the gun.

"Please," she whispered. "Let me help you."

For a moment, Jack thought it might work. He saw the tears forming in Lucas's eyes, saw the crack in his armor, the human being peeking through the monster's mask.

Then Lucas's face twisted, and he shoved her away. "You can't help me! No one can! I'm too far gone, Sarah. Too broken. Too much of what they made me."

He raised the gun again, but this time it wasn't pointed at Jack. It was pointed at his own head.

"Lucas, no!"

"Tell Daniel I said hello," Lucas whispered. "In whatever hell he's burning in."

The shot echoed through the mill like thunder, and everything went silent.
