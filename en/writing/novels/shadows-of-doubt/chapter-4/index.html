Shadows of Doubt
Chapter 4: A Father's Shadow
March 10, 2026
4,500 words · Chapter 4 of 20

The garage where Jack's father had spent thirty years of his life smelled of motor oil and memory. Morrison's Auto Repair hadn't changed in the decade since Michael Morrison's death—the same hydraulic lifts, the same rusted tool chests, the same calendar from 2009 pinned to the wall, forever frozen in November.

Jack stood in the doorway, watching dust motes dance in the afternoon light that filtered through grimy windows. He hadn't been back here since the funeral. Some doors, he had told himself, were better left closed.

But The Machinist had changed that.

"Your father tried to warn them, Jack. But they silenced him. Just like they're trying to silence you."

The text message had arrived at 3:00 AM, waking him from dreams he couldn't remember but couldn't shake. Now, standing in his father's kingdom of grease and steel, Jack wondered what else he didn't know about the man who had raised him.

"Detective Morrison?"

The voice came from the back office. Artie Brennan emerged, wiping his hands on a rag that had seen better decades. He had worked alongside Michael Morrison for twenty years, had been the one to find him when the heart attack took him at fifty-six, and had kept the garage running out of some loyalty Jack had never quite understood.

"Artie. Thanks for meeting me."

"You said it was about your father." Artie's eyes—watery with age but still sharp—studied Jack with uncomfortable intensity. "Thought you didn't want nothing to do with this place. Or him."

The accusation hung in the air, deserved and undeniable. Jack had been twenty-five when his father died, already a cop, already carrying secrets that had built walls between them. He had come to the funeral, stood by the grave, and had never returned.

"I need to know about the mill," Jack said. "November 2009. The week before the explosion."

Artie's face went gray. He turned away, busying himself with a wrench that didn't need adjusting. "That was a long time ago, Jackie. Your father's gone. Let him rest."

"I can't." Jack pulled out his phone, showed Artie the text from The Machinist. "Someone's killing people connected to that explosion. Three already. And they're sending me messages about my father. About things he did. Things he knew."

Artie's hands stopped moving. For a long moment, the only sound was the hum of the fluorescent lights overhead. Then: "Close the door. Lock it."

They sat in the back office, surrounded by decades of accumulated clutter—parts catalogs, faded Polaroids, a coffee mug that read "World's Okayest Mechanic." Artie lit a cigarette, something Jack hadn't seen him do in years, and began to speak in a voice barely above a whisper.

"Your father did work out at Harrison Steel sometimes. Private contract. The bosses liked him because he didn't ask questions and he didn't talk union." Artie exhaled smoke toward the ceiling. "Two weeks before the explosion, he came back from a job out there looking like he'd seen a ghost. Wouldn't eat. Wouldn't sleep. Sat in this very chair and stared at that wall for three hours straight."

"What did he see?"

"He wouldn't say. Not to me. But I heard him on the phone one night, yelling at someone. Something about 'methane levels' and 'people are going to die.' He said he was going to the safety board in Pittsburgh. Said someone had to listen."

Jack felt cold despite the summer heat. "Did he go?"

"He tried." Artie crushed out the cigarette with more force than necessary. "Three days before the explosion, two men came to see him. Suits. Harrison Steel security, or that's what they claimed. They were here for two hours. When they left, your father was different. Quieter. Scared, though he'd never admit it."

"What did they say to him?"

"I don't know. But I know what they left behind." Artie reached under the desk, pulled out a metal strongbox that Jack recognized from childhood. "Your father told me to hold onto this. Said if anything ever happened to him, I should give it to you when you were ready. I guess that time is now."

The box contained photographs, reports, and a single handwritten letter in Michael Morrison's familiar blocky script.

Jack— If you're reading this, then I failed. I failed to stop what was coming, and I failed to be the father you needed. But I'm begging you, don't let them bury this. Harrison knew about the methane buildup. I saw the inspection reports. I heard the foreman talking about 'cost-cutting measures.' They chose profit over people, son. They always do. I tried to warn them. I tried to go to the authorities. But they have friends in high places. They told me if I spoke, they'd make sure you never made detective. They'd make sure your career ended before it began. I was weak. I chose you over twenty-three strangers, and I've had to live with that choice every day since. The explosion wasn't an accident. It was murder dressed up as negligence. And the men responsible are still walking around, still rich, still powerful. Don't be like me, Jackie. Don't stay silent. Make them pay. Dad

Jack read the letter three times, each word burning into his memory. His hands shook. His father—his quiet, hardworking, never-made-waves father—had known. Had tried to stop it. Had been threatened into silence.

And Jack had spent ten years hating him for being distant, for never understanding why Jack became a cop, for dying before they could fix what was broken between them.

"There are photos in there too," Artie said quietly. "Your father took them the last time he was out at the mill. The methane detectors. You can see the wires were cut."

Jack spread the photographs across the desk. Grainy, shot from a distance, but clear enough. Safety equipment deliberately sabotaged. Warning systems disabled. And in one corner of the final image, a face Jack recognized from the news—William Harrison Sr., watching the work with a smile.

His phone buzzed. Another text from The Machinist: "Found the letter, I see. Your father was a hero, Jack. A flawed one, but a hero nonetheless. He tried to save them. You, on the other hand, have spent fifteen years looking the other way. Tell me—which one of you should feel ashamed?"

Jack typed back with fingers that felt numb: "Who are you?"

The reply was immediate: "I'm the voice of the twenty-three. I'm the weight of their unavenged deaths. And I'm disappointed, Jack. Your father sacrificed his conscience for your future. And you've squandered it on whiskey and self-pity. But it's not too late. Join me. Help me finish what he started."

Jack stared at the message, fury and grief warring in his chest. "I'm going to find you," he typed. "And when I do, I'm going to put you in a cage where you belong."

The Machinist's response was almost tender: "You sound just like him. Stubborn to a fault. See you soon, Jackie."

Jack pocketed the phone and gathered the evidence—his father's legacy, his shame, his last chance at redemption. Artie watched him with eyes that had seen too much.

"He loved you, Jackie. Whatever else he did or didn't do, he loved you more than anything. Don't forget that."

Outside, the sun was setting over Ashford, painting the dying town in shades of rust and gold. Jack stood beside his car, the box of his father's secrets heavy in his hands, and made a silent promise to the ghost of Michael Morrison.

He would finish what his father started. He would expose the truth about Harrison Steel. And he would catch The Machinist, even if it meant becoming the very thing he hunted.

The phone rang. Sarah Chen's name appeared on the screen.

"Jack, I found something. Elite Restoration Services—the cleaning company that hit Harrison's office? Their payment trail leads to a law firm. Harrison, Pierce & Associates. The senior partner is Marcus Pierce, and he's been Harrison family's personal attorney for thirty years."

Jack felt the pieces clicking into place. "And Pierce would have access to all the old case files. He'd know who to silence, who to protect."

"There's more," Sarah said, and he could hear the excitement in her voice. "I found a witness. The safety inspector who was supposed to certify the methane detectors in 2009. His name is Walter Dunn. He's alive, Jack. And he says he's willing to talk."

Jack closed his eyes, feeling the weight of fifteen years beginning to shift. "Where is he?"

"A cabin in the Poconos. He moved there after the explosion. Changed his name, disappeared. But I found him. We can be there by tomorrow morning."

"Pick me up in an hour."

Jack ended the call and looked back at the garage where his father had spent his life, where secrets had been kept and promises broken. The shadows were lengthening now, creeping across the oil-stained concrete like fingers reaching for something to hold onto.

Tomorrow, he would meet the man who might finally give him answers. Tomorrow, he would learn if justice was possible in a town built on lies.

But tonight, he sat on the hood of his car and watched the stars emerge one by one over Ashford, thinking of his father and the silence between them that had lasted too long.

"I'm sorry, Dad," he whispered to the darkness. "And I'm going to make it right."

The darkness didn't answer. But somewhere in the town below, Jack knew, The Machinist was listening.
