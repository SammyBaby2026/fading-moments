Shadows of Doubt
Chapter 1: The First Gear
March 7, 2026
4,500 words · Chapter 1 of 20

The rain fell on Ashford like God's own tears, washing nothing clean. Jack Morrison stood at the edge of the crime scene, watching the water carry blood down the gutter in thin, diluted streams. It was the kind of Midwestern night that made you question your life choices—the wind cutting through your coat, the smell of rust and decay in the air, the knowledge that somewhere warm, people were living lives that didn't involve staring at dead bodies.

But that wasn't his life. His life was here, in the shadow of the old Harrison Steel Mill, where the American Dream had gone to die fifteen years ago along with twenty-three night-shift workers.

"Detective Morrison." The uniformed officer approached, trying to keep the rain off his notepad and failing. "Medical examiner's on the way. And... there's a reporter already sniffing around."

Jack didn't turn. "Who?"

"Sarah Chen. From the Gazette."

That made him turn. Sarah Chen had been a pain in his ass for three years now—ever since she'd returned to Ashford from Chicago with a journalism degree and a chip on her shoulder. She'd written pieces on police corruption, the opioid epidemic, and last year, a four-part series on the Harrison explosion that the town's elite had tried to bury. She was smart, relentless, and absolutely convinced that Ashford PD was either incompetent or corrupt.

She was also the daughter of Marcus Chen, one of the twenty-three who died in the mill.

"Keep her back," Jack said. "This isn't a goddamn press conference."

But even as he spoke, he saw her—small frame wrapped in a black raincoat, camera slung around her neck, already talking to one of the younger officers who looked like he was about to give her his life story. She caught Jack's eye across the distance, and he saw the challenge in her gaze. The unspoken accusation: What are you hiding, Morrison?

He turned back to the body.

Dr. William Harrison lay on the wet pavement like a broken puppet, his skull cracked open from the fall. He'd jumped—or been pushed—from his office window on the fourteenth floor of the Harrison Building, the last monument to his family's faded empire. The irony wasn't lost on Jack: a Harrison dying in the shadow of the mill that had made his family rich and ultimately destroyed them.

"Suicide note?" Jack asked the officer.

"None found. But his office door was locked from the inside. Window was open."

The wages of sin is death.

The answer came fifteen minutes later, when the new medical examiner—a young woman named Dr. Patel, fresh from the Cleveland office—knelt beside the body and carefully opened the dead man's clenched right hand.

"Detective?" Her voice was steady, professional, but Jack caught the tension in it. "You need to see this."

He knelt beside her, ignoring the rainwater soaking through his pants. In Harrison's palm lay a gear—small, rusted, clearly old. Industrial. The kind that would have been used in heavy machinery decades ago.

On its surface, scratched with something sharp, was a number: 15.

Fifteen years.

Jack felt the familiar coldness in his gut, the same feeling he'd had when the first "accident" happened three months ago. Then the second, two weeks ago. Both former mill workers. Both ruled accidents by the previous medical examiner—Dr. William Harrison himself.

"Bag it," he said quietly. "And don't mention it to anyone."

Dr. Patel looked at him with sharp, intelligent eyes. "This is the third one, isn't it? With the gear?"

Jack said nothing.

"I read the files when I transferred," she continued. "Two accidental deaths in the past three months. Both former Harrison Steel employees. Both found with mechanical components on their persons. The previous ME—" she nodded at the corpse "—ruled them accidents. But you filed supplemental reports questioning the findings."

"You read too much, Doctor."

"And you know this isn't a suicide."

Jack stood, his knees popping. "I know that Dr. Harrison had access to every death certificate in this county for the past thirty years. I know he was the ME on duty the night of the mill explosion. And I know that twenty-three men died that night, and someone thinks they deserve justice that the courts never delivered."

He looked up at the Harrison Building, dark against the rainy sky. Fourteenth floor. The window was still open, curtains billowing like ghostly fingers beckoning.

"Twenty dead," he corrected himself. "Twenty-three died in the explosion. Three survived."

"Including you?" Sarah Chen's voice came from behind him.

Jack turned slowly. She'd gotten past the cordon, of course she had. Her dark hair was plastered to her face, rain running down her cheeks like tears she wouldn't shed.

"You were there that night," she said. It wasn't a question. "You were the night watchman. The only survivor they could explain—you were outside having a smoke when the boilers blew."

"Get off my crime scene, Ms. Chen."

"Two of the survivors are dead now. Accidents, they said." She took a step closer, ignoring the uniformed officer who moved to intervene. Jack waved him off. "But you don't believe that. You never did."

"I believe in evidence."

"Then you'll want to see this." She held out her phone. On the screen was an email—sent to Dr. Harrison forty-eight hours ago. The subject line read: The wages of sin is death. The body: You signed twenty-three death certificates. Tonight you sign your own. The Machinist.

Jack took the phone. His hands were steady. They'd been steady since that night fifteen years ago, when he'd learned what it meant to survive while everyone else died.

"Where did you get this?"

"Harrison forwarded it to me. He was... scared. He wanted to talk." Her voice caught slightly. "He knew something about the explosion. Something he'd kept secret all these years."

The Machinist, Jack thought. A name from the industrial past, when Ashford had been a city of steel and coal instead of opioids and despair. Someone who understood machines. Someone who knew how to make murder look like accident.

Someone who remembered.

Jack looked at the gear in the evidence bag, at the number scratched into its rusted surface. He thought about the twenty dead men—good men, working men, men who'd had families and dreams and futures until a spark in a methane pocket ended everything. He thought about the three survivors: himself, a man named Earl Thompson who'd died in a "hunting accident" three months ago, and a woman named Patricia Kowalski who'd fallen down her basement stairs two weeks ago.

Now Dr. Harrison, who'd ruled both deaths accidental.

Twenty-three minus three survivors equals twenty. Plus the three who'd been killed since...

Twenty-three.

Someone was balancing the books.

Jack handed Sarah Chen her phone. "You need to leave. Now."

"I'm not going anywhere. My father died in that mill. If someone is finally telling the truth—"

"If someone is killing everyone connected to that night," Jack interrupted, "then you're in danger just by being here. By talking to me. By knowing what you know."

He leaned in close enough to smell the rain on her skin, the faint trace of coffee on her breath.

"The Machinist isn't done." He kept his voice low, just between them. "Three down, twenty to go. And if you're not careful, Ms. Chen, you'll be twenty-four."

He walked away before she could respond, toward the flashing lights of the ambulance that would carry Dr. Harrison's body to the morgue. Behind him, the Harrison Steel Mill loomed against the night sky—a skeleton of rusted girders and broken windows, a monument to greed and grief.

Somewhere in that darkness, The Machinist was watching. Waiting.

And Jack Morrison, the man who should have died fifteen years ago, finally understood that the past wasn't done with him. Not by a long shot.

The game, it seemed, had just begun.
