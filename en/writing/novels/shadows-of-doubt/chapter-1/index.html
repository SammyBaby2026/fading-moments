Chapter 1: The First Gear

March 7, 2026 · 4,200 words · Ongoing

The rain fell on Jiangcheng like a veil of secrets, washing the ancient streets clean of their sins while simultaneously obscuring new ones. It was the kind of night that made you believe ghosts might actually exist—not the transparent kind from childhood stories, but the heavy, breathing kind that carried regret in their pockets and whispered names in the dark.

Lin Mo stood at the edge of the rooftop, watching the city dissolve into watercolor below. Forty floors up, the wind carried the smell of wet concrete and something else—something metallic that reminded him of the factory where his father had worked thirty years ago. Where he himself had worked one summer, before the explosion changed everything.

"Captain." The voice came from behind him, hesitant, young. Detective Zhang, barely twenty-six, still believed that dead bodies meant something. Still believed that justice was a destination rather than a direction. "The medical examiner is here."

Lin Mo didn't turn around. "Tell him to wait."

"It's... it's a her, actually. New hire from the provincial office."

That made Lin Mo turn. The provincial office didn't send people to Jiangcheng unless someone important had died, or someone important was asking questions. He walked toward the stairwell, his shoes squeaking on the wet concrete, and descended into the fluorescent-lit corridor where his life was about to change.

The body lay in apartment 3804, a corner unit that should have commanded a premium view of the river but instead looked down on a parking lot where a single streetlamp flickered its death throes. The deceased was Chen Weimin, sixty-two, retired forensic pathologist, widower, collector of antique clocks.

He had fallen from his own balcony. Or jumped. Or been pushed.

The new medical examiner was kneeling beside the body, her dark hair pulled back in a severe bun that seemed designed to project professionalism. She didn't look up when Lin Mo entered. "Captain Lin. I've heard about you."

"Good things, I hope."

"That you don't believe in accidents." She finally looked up, and Lin Mo saw she was younger than he'd expected—perhaps thirty, with eyes that had already seen too much. "I'm Dr. Su. Su Li."

The name hit him like a physical blow. Su. There had been a Su in the factory records. A supervisor who died in the explosion, leaving behind a daughter. Lin Mo felt the familiar tightness in his chest, the ghost of panic that had lived there since he was twenty.

"What can you tell me about Dr. Chen?" he asked, forcing his voice steady.

Su Li stood, wiping her hands on a cloth that left no residue. "Officially? He fell from his balcony at approximately 11 PM. The impact killed him instantly. There are no defensive wounds, no signs of struggle in the apartment."

"And unofficially?"

She held up an evidence bag containing something small and metallic. "He was holding this when he died. Clutched it so tightly it left indentations in his palm."

Lin Mo took the bag. Inside was a gear—a small, rusted gear from some kind of machinery. It looked old, perhaps decades old. On its surface, barely visible, someone had scratched a number: 15.

"Any idea what it means?" Su Li asked.

Fifteen years. That's what it meant. Fifteen years since the explosion at the Jiangcheng Chemical Plant. Fifteen years since twenty-three people died, their deaths ruled an accident caused by negligence. Fifteen years since Lin Mo had stood in the rain, watching the flames consume the building where his father had worked for twenty years.

"No," Lin Mo lied. "No idea at all."

But even as he spoke, his eyes were drawn to the window, to the city beyond, to the shadows that seemed suddenly deeper, hungrier. Chen Weimin had been the forensic pathologist on duty that night fifteen years ago. He had signed the death certificates. He had ruled it an accident.

And now he was dead, clutching a gear from a factory that no longer existed.

Lin Mo's phone buzzed in his pocket. A text message from an unknown number. He opened it with a sense of dread that felt like premonition.

"The first of the guilty has fallen. Twenty-two remain. Do you remember the rain, Captain Lin? I do. I remember everything."

Lin Mo looked up to find Su Li watching him with those too-knowing eyes. "Everything alright?"

"No," he said quietly. "I don't think it is."

Outside, the rain continued to fall, washing the city clean while somewhere in the darkness, someone was planning the next death. Someone who remembered. Someone who had been waiting fifteen years for justice that the law had failed to deliver.

Lin Mo thought of the gear in the evidence bag, of the number scratched into its rusted surface. He thought of his father, who had come home from the factory that night smelling of chemicals and fear. He thought of the sealed files in the basement of the municipal building, files that no one was allowed to access.

Most of all, he thought of the twenty-three dead, and he wondered which of them had left behind someone patient enough to wait fifteen years for revenge.

The game, it seemed, had just begun.
