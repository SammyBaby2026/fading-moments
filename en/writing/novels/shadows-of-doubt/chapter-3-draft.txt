
Chapter 3: The Locked Room
The nursing home smelled of antiseptic and regret, a chemical sweetness that couldn't quite mask the underlying scent of decay. Jack Morrison stood in the lobby of the Ashford Senior Care Center, watching an old man in a wheelchair stare out the window at a parking lot where nothing interesting ever happened.

Richard Holloway had been a lion once. In the nineties, he'd taken on Harrison Steel's legal team expecting to lose, but he'd fought anyway—because someone had to, because twenty-three families deserved better than a sealed settlement and a nondisclosure agreement. Now he was seventy-three years old, his mind dissolving like sugar in rain, and the case files that might have held answers were scattered to the winds.

Or so everyone thought.

"You're Detective Morrison?" The woman approaching him was in her forties, with Holloway's sharp cheekbones and tired eyes. "I'm Elizabeth Holloway-Morris. My father's daughter."

"Thank you for seeing me, Ms. Holloway-Morris."

"It's about the steel mill case, isn't it?" She didn't wait for confirmation. "Everyone thinks that case died with my father's mind. But he was paranoid, Detective. The good kind of paranoid—the kind that makes you keep backup copies of everything."

Jack felt a flicker of hope, dangerous as a match in a gas leak. "You have the files?"

"Not here." She glanced toward the desk nurse, lowering her voice. "My father knew someone might come looking one day. He made me promise to keep them safe. Follow me."

---

The Holloway & Associates law office occupied the second floor of a Victorian building on Main Street, sandwiched between a bankruptcy attorney and a payday loan shop. The irony wasn't lost on Jack—justice and desperation, sharing the same block.

Elizabeth unlocked a fire safe hidden behind a bookshelf in her father's old office. "He stopped practicing five years ago when the Alzheimer's started getting bad. But he made me promise—if anyone ever asked about the Harrison case, I was to give them access to the backup files."

"Why me? Why now?"

"Because three people connected to that explosion are dead in the last three months, Detective. I read the news." She pulled out a metal file box, old but well-maintained. "And because two days ago, someone broke into this office. They didn't take anything—they were looking for something specific."

Jack's pulse quickened. "What were they looking for?"

"This." She opened the box, revealing folders organized by year, by case, by client. "The complete Harrison Steel wrongful death files. Including the ones my father never filed."

She handed him a folder marked "CONFIDENTIAL—DO NOT FILE" in her father's familiar handwriting. Inside were witness statements that had never made it to court, photographs that had never been entered into evidence, and a single audio cassette labeled "V. Kowalski—Deathbed Statement, March 2019."

Vincent Kowalski. Patricia's husband. The man who'd worked the night shift for fifteen years and died claiming he had proof the explosion wasn't an accident.

"There's a player in the desk," Elizabeth said quietly. "I think you need to hear this."

---

The tape hissed for a moment, then a man's voice emerged—weak, breathless, but with an urgency that transcended the poor recording quality.

"This is Vincent Kowalski. I worked the night of November 14, 2009. I was there when the boilers blew. And I know what really happened."

Jack leaned closer, forgetting to breathe.

"Harrison Sr. knew about the methane buildup. We all knew. The detectors had been faulty for weeks, but maintenance was 'deferred' until after the quarterly inspection. We were told to keep working or lose our jobs. Some of us complained. I complained—wrote a letter to the safety board. Nothing happened."

A wet cough, then silence. Jack thought the tape had ended, but then Vincent's voice returned, even weaker now.

"But that's not the worst part. The worst part is what happened after. The investigation was fixed. The safety inspector was paid off—I saw the envelope myself, in Harrison's office, two days before the 'official' report cleared the company. And the workers who tried to talk? They were threatened. Some disappeared. Marcus Chen—Sarah's father—he was going to go to the press. Then he died in the explosion. Convenient, wasn't it?"

Another long pause. Jack could hear hospital machines in the background, the beep of a heart monitor counting down borrowed time.

"I've got proof. Copies of the payoff ledger, photos of the faulty equipment before the blast, a statement from the inspector before he died of 'natural causes' in 2015. I'm giving everything to Dr. Harrison. He was the medical examiner. He knows how to hide things where the company can't find them."

The tape ended with a click that seemed to echo in the quiet office.

Elizabeth was crying silently, tears tracking down her cheeks. "My father never filed this. He was going to—but then Vincent died, and Dr. Harrison said he couldn't find the evidence, and the case was already settled, and... and he was afraid. Harrison Steel owned this town. They still do, even with Harrison Sr. dead."

"Not for much longer," Jack said quietly. He was thinking about the cleaning crew at Harrison's office, about the Machinist who knew too much, about his own father who'd apparently "tried to warn them." The picture was coming together, piece by bloody piece.

"There's one more thing," Elizabeth said, wiping her eyes. She pulled a second folder from the box—thinner, newer, dated 2019. "After Vincent died, my father started his own investigation. He tracked down the families of the twenty-three victims. He was building a new case, planning to file a class action suit with fresh evidence. But then..."

"Then the Alzheimer's started."

"Then the Alzheimer's started," she confirmed. "And the threats. Someone broke into his house, Detective. Last year, while he was sleeping. They didn't take anything. They just... rearranged things. Moved his pills around. Left a gear from a steel mill on his pillow."

Jack felt ice in his veins. The same signature. The same message.

"He's been watching us all this time," Jack said. "The Machinist. He knows who was responsible. He knows who covered it up. And he's been waiting for someone to start asking questions again."

"What are you going to do?"

Jack closed the file box, his mind racing. "I'm going to find out what really happened to my father. I'm going to stop a killer before he reaches target number four. And I'm going to make sure that twenty-three people didn't die for nothing."

He stood, tucking the files under his arm. "Can I borrow these?"

"They're yours. My father would have wanted you to have them." She paused at the door, looking back at him. "Detective? Be careful. Whoever killed those people—whoever killed Vincent and Dr. Harrison—they're not just some crazy vigilante. They're organized. They're patient. And they're very, very angry."

Jack nodded, already planning his next move. He needed to find out what his father had known. He needed to talk to Sarah, show her the files, see if she recognized any of the names in her father's old letters. He needed—

His phone buzzed. A text from an unknown number.

"You found the files. Good. But files won't bring back the dead, Jack. Only justice can do that. And justice requires sacrifice. Are you willing to sacrifice everything to learn the truth about your father? Are you willing to become what I am? —M"

Jack stared at the message, his blood running cold. The Machinist wasn't just watching the investigation. He was watching Jack, specifically. Targeting him. Trying to recruit him or break him—Jack wasn't sure which yet.

He typed back a response before he could think better of it: "Who are you really?"

The reply came in seconds: "I'm the ghost of Ashford's conscience. I'm what happens when justice is denied. And soon, Jack—very soon—you'll have to choose which side you're on. Because there are no innocent bystanders in this war. Only victims and avengers."

Jack pocketed the phone, his hands shaking slightly. He walked out of the law office into the gray afternoon, the weight of the files heavy under his arm and the weight of the future heavier still on his shoulders.

Twenty-three dead. Three murdered since. And a killer who thought he was doing justice.

The rain started again, cold and persistent, washing the streets clean of nothing. Jack Morrison pulled his collar up against the wind and walked toward his car, toward the truth, toward a confrontation he could feel coming like thunder before a storm.

The Machinist was out there, watching, waiting. But now Jack had something he hadn't had before.

He had proof. He had names. He had the beginning of an answer.

And he was done being a victim.

The game was changing. And this time, Jack intended to win.
