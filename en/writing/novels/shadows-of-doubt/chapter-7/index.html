Shadows of Doubt
Chapter 7: The Mechanic from Detroit
March 13, 2026
4,500 words · Chapter 7 of 20

Detroit in winter was a city of gray—gray sky, gray streets, gray buildings that seemed to sag under the weight of their own history. Jack had been here once before, years ago, tracking a fugitive who'd fled across state lines. He remembered thinking then that Detroit felt like a warning of what happened when the world moved on and left you behind.

He still thought so now.

"Robert Hayes, sixty-two years old, last known address in Hamtramck," Sarah read from her laptop as they drove through streets lined with abandoned factories and burned-out houses. "He worked as a maintenance technician at Harrison Steel from 2005 until the explosion. According to the DMV records, he renewed his license three years ago at a AAA office on Woodward Avenue."

"Any credit card activity? Bank records?"

"Nothing. Either he's living completely off the grid, or he's using a false identity." Sarah closed her laptop. "But people like Hayes—skilled tradesmen, mechanical experts—they don't just disappear. They need work. Tools. Supplies. Someone in this city knows where he is."

Their first stop was a mechanics' supply shop on the east side, the kind of place that sold parts for industrial equipment and asked no questions. The owner, a wiry man named Deshawn with grease permanently embedded under his fingernails, looked at the photo Jack showed him and shook his head.

"Don't know him."

"Are you sure? He would have bought parts for methane detection systems, safety equipment—"

"I said I don't know him." Deshawn's eyes shifted to the door, then back. "And if I were you, I'd stop asking."

Jack followed his gaze. A black van was parked across the street, its windows tinted, its engine running. No one sat in the driver's seat.

"When did they get here?" Jack asked quietly.

"Ten minutes before you did." Deshawn leaned closer. "Look, man, I don't want trouble. Whoever that is, they've been asking about the same guy. Showing his picture around. Leaving notes in mailboxes."

"What kind of notes?"

"The kind with a gear on them." Deshawn tapped his counter. "You know what that means. Everyone around here knows. It means The Machinist is hunting."

Jack and Sarah exchanged glances. They were too late—The Machinist had already been here, already sown fear through the community.

"We can protect you," Sarah said. "If you know where Hayes is, tell us. We can get to him before The Machinist does."

Deshawn laughed, a harsh, humorless sound. "Lady, nobody protects anyone from that psycho. Four people dead in Pennsylvania, all of them 'accidents.' Cops can't touch him. You can't touch him. The only thing you can do is stay out of his way."

"We're not going to stay out of his way." Jack placed his card on the counter. "If you change your mind, call me. Anytime, day or night."

They spent the next six hours driving through Detroit's industrial wasteland, visiting machine shops, salvage yards, and underground garages. Everywhere they went, they found the same thing—fear, silence, and sometimes, a rusted gear left where Hayes might have worked.

"He's playing with us," Sarah said as they sat in a diner, nursing cold coffee. "Leaving those gears where he knows we'll find them. He's telling us he knows more than we do."

"Or he's telling us we're close." Jack stirred his coffee, watching the dark swirl. "If he's leaving markers, it's because Hayes is still alive. The Machinist hasn't found him yet."

"But he's narrowing the search. Every person we talk to, every dead end we hit—The Machinist learns something too."

Jack's phone buzzed. Unknown number. He answered on the second ring.

"You're persistent, Jackie. I'll give you that."

The voice was different this time—not the mechanical filter from before, but something rawer, more human. Still disguised, but less careful.

"The Machinist," Jack said, putting the call on speaker so Sarah could hear. "You wanted to talk?"

"I wanted to warn you. You're walking into something you don't understand."

"Then help me understand."

Silence on the line. Then: "Drive east on Gratiot Avenue. Five miles past the airport, there's an abandoned Chrysler plant. Be there in thirty minutes. Come alone, or I disappear and Hayes dies in the dark like all the others."

"And if I come?"

"Then we talk. Face to face. Just you and me."

The line went dead.

"No way," Sarah said immediately. "It's a trap."

"Of course it's a trap." Jack stood, throwing bills on the table. "But he's right about one thing—if I don't go, Hayes dies. And we lose our only witness."

"Then I'm coming with you."

"He said alone."

"Jack—"

"Sarah." He turned to her, putting his hands on her shoulders. "I need you to do something else. Go to the Detroit PD, talk to their organized crime unit. Tell them about the gears, about The Machinist, about everything. If something happens to me, they'll need to know where to start."

"Nothing's going to happen to you."

"Promise me."

She searched his face, then nodded slowly. "I promise."

The abandoned Chrysler plant loomed against the gray sky, a cathedral of rust and broken glass. Jack parked his car a hundred yards away and approached on foot, his hand near his weapon but not drawing it. He was alone, unarmed except for the pistol at his hip and the recorder in his pocket.

The main entrance gaped open like a missing tooth. Jack stepped inside, his shoes crunching on debris—shattered glass, fragments of metal, the detritus of twenty years of abandonment.

"Hello, Jackie."

The voice came from above. Jack looked up to see a figure silhouetted against the broken skylights, standing on a catwalk thirty feet overhead. The figure was average height, average build—utterly unremarkable except for the stillness of his posture.

"The Machinist," Jack said.

"You can call me that." The figure didn't move. "I like the name the press gave me. It sounds industrial. Mechanical. Like clockwork."

"Where's Hayes?"

"Safe. For now." The Machinist stepped forward, and for the first time, Jack could see his face—a plain face, the kind you'd forget in a crowd, with dark eyes that held no warmth. He wore coveralls and work boots, the uniform of a thousand factory workers. "I needed to talk to you first. Needed you to understand before you saw him."

"Understand what?"

"What this is really about." The Machinist leaned on the railing, looking down at Jack. "You think I'm a monster. A serial killer with delusions of justice. And maybe I am. But I'm also something else—I'm the only one who remembers. The only one who cares."

"I care," Jack said. "I've spent fifteen years—"

"Fifteen years doing what? Drinking? Running? Pretending you're not the same coward who let twenty-three men die?" The Machinist's voice was calm, almost sympathetic. "I know you, Jackie. Better than you know yourself. I know about the guilt. The nightmares. The way you check the stove three times before bed because you can't trust your own perception anymore."

Jack felt his blood run cold. "How—"

"I've been watching you for years. Since before I started killing. Since before I knew what I would become." The Machinist descended a staircase, moving with practiced grace. "You were my first choice, Jackie. My template. The son of a hero who became a coward. I thought if I could understand you, I could understand what happened to my father."

"Your father was at the mill."

"My father was David Chen."

The name hit Jack like a physical blow. David Chen—Sarah's father. The man whose death had driven her to become a journalist, to search for truth.

"You're Sarah's..."

"Brother." The Machinist—David Chen's son—stepped into the light, and Jack could see the resemblance now. The same dark eyes, the same sharp cheekbones. "Half-brother, technically. Different mothers. My mom died when I was two. Dad raised me alone until he met Sarah's mother. I was nine when the explosion happened."

"Sarah doesn't know."

"Sarah thinks I died in a car accident five years ago." The Machinist—Jack couldn't think of him that way anymore, not now—smiled sadly. "It was easier that way. Easier to become a ghost than to explain why I needed to disappear."

"Why are you telling me this?"

"Because you're going to help me." He reached into his coveralls and withdrew a folded piece of paper. "Hayes is in the basement. He's alive, but he won't be for long if he doesn't get medical attention. He told me everything—how Marcus Pierce threatened him, how he was forced to recalibrate the detectors, how Pierce paid him fifty thousand dollars to disappear after the explosion."

Jack took the paper. It was a handwritten confession, signed by Robert Hayes, dated and witnessed.

"This is enough to arrest Pierce."

"It's enough to do more than arrest him." The younger Chen's eyes glittered. "But you need to make a choice, Jackie. You can take Hayes to the hospital, save his life, and use this confession to build your case. Or you can let him die and give me Pierce. Justice without the law's delays."

"You want me to let him die?"

"I want you to understand that some debts can only be paid in blood." He turned toward a door at the back of the factory. "Hayes is through there. He's been beaten—my handiwork, I'm afraid. I needed to be sure he was telling the truth. He has maybe two hours before internal bleeding kills him."

"You're insane."

"I'm consistent." He walked back toward the stairs. "Call your ambulance, Jackie. Be the hero your father was. But remember this—Pierce won't stop. He has connections in the state government, the courts, the police. He'll stall, he'll appeal, he'll walk. And when he does, I'll be waiting."

"And if I stop you?"

The figure paused at the top of the stairs, looking back. "Then you'll have to kill me. And I don't think you have it in you. Not yet."

He disappeared into the shadows.

Jack ran for the door, bursting through to find Robert Hayes tied to a chair in a makeshift operating room. The old man's face was swollen almost beyond recognition, his breathing shallow and wet.

"Help is coming," Jack said, pulling out his phone. "Stay with me, Mr. Hayes. Tell me what you told him. Tell me everything."

Hayes's eyes fluttered open. "Pierce... he made me do it. Threatened my family. Said if I talked, he'd..." He coughed, blood flecking his lips. "I have proof. Bank records. Hidden... in my shop..."

"Where's your shop?"

"1432... Michigan Avenue. Behind the... false wall..." His head lolled forward. "I'm sorry. So sorry. I didn't want... those men to die..."

"I know. I know you didn't." Jack held his hand while calling 911, listening to Hayes's breathing grow weaker. "Stay with me. Please. We need you to testify."

But Hayes was unconscious by the time the ambulance arrived. Jack watched them load him into the vehicle, the confession clutched in his hand, and knew that he had crossed a line somewhere today.

He had looked into the eyes of a killer and seen himself.

The drive to Michigan Avenue took twenty minutes. Jack found the shop easily—a nondescript garage between a liquor store and a vacant lot. The door was locked, but a single kick from his steel-toed boot solved that problem.

Behind the false wall, exactly where Hayes had said, Jack found a safe. Inside were bank records, transfer slips showing payments from a Harrison Steel account to Hayes's personal account, totaling exactly fifty thousand dollars. And a note, written in Pierce's distinctive hand: "For services rendered. Your silence is appreciated."

Jack photographed everything, then called Sarah.

"We have him," he said. "Pierce is finished."

"Hayes?"

"In surgery. Touch and go." Jack looked at the evidence spread before him. "But we don't need him to testify. We have enough to arrest Pierce right now."

"Jack, there's something else." Sarah's voice was strange, tight. "I did some digging while you were gone. Marcus Pierce isn't just a former executive. He's connected—deeply connected—to state politics. His brother is a state senator. His cousin sits on the appeals court."

"I don't care if his uncle is the President."

"You should care. Because I also found something else." A pause. "Pierce knows you're coming. He knows about Detroit, about Hayes, about everything. Someone tipped him off."

Jack felt ice form in his stomach. "Who?"

"I don't know. But Jack—be careful. This isn't just about the explosion anymore. Someone powerful is protecting Pierce, and they know your name."

Jack gathered the evidence and walked out into the Detroit night. The city spread before him like a maze of shadows and secrets, and somewhere in those shadows, The Machinist—David Chen's son, Sarah's brother—was watching.

They had a confession. They had bank records. They had enough to put Marcus Pierce away for life.

But as Jack got into his car and started the engine, he couldn't shake the feeling that he'd been played. That every move he'd made had been anticipated, every choice predicted.

The game wasn't over.

It had only just begun.
