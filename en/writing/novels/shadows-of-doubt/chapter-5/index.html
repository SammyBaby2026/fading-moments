Shadows of Doubt
Chapter 5: The Witness Who Knew Too Much
March 11, 2026
4,500 words · Chapter 5 of 20

The Poconos in November were a study in dying beauty—trees stripped bare against gunmetal skies, lakes reflecting nothing but the promise of winter. Sarah Chen drove while Jack sat in the passenger seat of her battered Subaru, watching the world blur past at seventy miles per hour.

"Walter Dunn changed his name to Thomas Webb fifteen years ago," Sarah said, eyes fixed on the winding mountain road. "Bought the cabin with cash, pays his taxes through a shell company, hasn't been back to Ashford since the week after the explosion."

"If he's been hiding this long, why talk now?"

"Because I told him about Dr. Harrison. And Earl Thompson. And Patricia Kowalski." Sarah's knuckles were white on the steering wheel. "I told him that everyone who knew the truth was dying. He said he'd rather testify than be next."

Jack checked his phone. No signal. They were deep in the mountains now, far from cell towers and civilization. The perfect place to hide. Or to die.

"You think The Machinist knows about him?"

"I think The Machinist knows everything." Sarah's voice was flat, resigned. "But Dunn called me yesterday. He sounded scared but determined. He wants this to end, Jack. He wants to do the right thing, finally."

The cabin appeared around a curve—a weathered wooden structure perched on the edge of a ridge, smoke curling from its stone chimney. A red pickup truck sat in the gravel driveway, dust already settling on its hood.

Sarah parked beside the truck. They sat in silence for a moment, listening to the engine tick as it cooled.

"Jack." Sarah turned to face him, and he saw something in her eyes he hadn't expected—fear. "What if we're too late? What if he's already—"

"We don't know that." But Jack was already reaching for his service weapon, checking the magazine by habit. "Stay behind me. And if I tell you to run, you run."

The cabin's front door stood slightly ajar. Jack felt his stomach drop.

"Mr. Webb?" he called, identifying himself as police. "Mr. Webb, this is Detective Jack Morrison. We're here to help."

Silence. The kind of silence that made Jack's skin crawl.

He pushed the door open with his foot, weapon raised. The living room was empty—a fireplace burning low, a half-empty coffee cup on the table, a book open face-down on the arm of a chair. Signs of life interrupted.

"Kitchen clear," Sarah whispered from behind him.

They found Dunn in the bedroom. He lay on the floor beside his bed, eyes open and staring at the ceiling, a look of mild surprise frozen on his face. No blood. No obvious wounds. Just a dead man who looked like he had simply stopped living.

"Cardiac arrest," Jack said, but his voice lacked conviction. He knelt beside the body, checking for a pulse he already knew wasn't there. The skin was still warm. "He hasn't been dead long. An hour, maybe two."

Sarah had gone pale. "We were supposed to meet him three hours ago. He was alive when he called me."

Jack's phone buzzed. One bar of signal, just enough for a text. The number was familiar now.

You're getting predictable, Jack. Always chasing, never catching. I was here two hours ago. Poor Walter was so relieved to see a friendly face—someone who understood his guilt, his shame for staying silent all these years. I gave him a choice: take his secrets to the grave, or let me help him find peace. He chose peace. You should have moved faster. But then, you've always been a step behind, haven't you? First your father, now this. Some detective you turned out to be. —M

Jack's hands were shaking as he read the message. Not from fear—from rage.

"He was here," Jack said quietly. "Two hours ago. He killed Dunn and waited for us to find the body."

"How could he know?" Sarah's voice cracked. "I was so careful. I covered my tracks, used a burner phone—"

"He's not working alone." Jack stood, looking around the cabin for anything The Machinist might have left behind. "Or he's tapped into something we don't understand. Police databases, phone records, something."

The phone rang.

Unknown number. Jack answered on the second ring.

"I see you found my handiwork." The voice was filtered, robotic, but Jack could hear the satisfaction in it. "Walter went peacefully, Jack. I want you to know that. No pain. Just... cessation. A heart that decided fifteen years of guilt was enough."

"You're a murderer," Jack said. "Nothing more."

"And you're a disappointment." The Machinist's voice carried genuine regret. "I had such hopes for you, Jackie. The son of a man who tried to do the right thing. I thought maybe, just maybe, you'd understand why this is necessary."

"I understand you're a killer who thinks he's righteous. I've put away dozens like you."

Laughter, distorted and strange. "You have no idea who I am. What I've lost. What I'm willing to lose."

"Then tell me."

A pause. Jack could hear breathing, steady and controlled.

"November 14, 2009. I was seventeen years old. My father was working the night shift at Harrison Steel—overtime he couldn't afford to turn down because my mother was sick and the medical bills were drowning us." The Machinist's voice had changed, losing its robotic quality, becoming something raw and human. "He called me at 11:47 PM. Said something was wrong, that the alarms were going off, that he loved me. The line went dead. When I got to the mill, it was already burning. They wouldn't let me close enough to see. They told me to go home, that they'd call when they knew something."

Jack felt the floor shift beneath him. "Your father was one of the twenty-three."

"His name was David Chen." The silence that followed was deafening. "Ring any bells, Detective?"

Jack looked at Sarah. Her face had gone from pale to ghostly white. She mouthed one word: "Uncle."

"You knew," Jack said into the phone. "You knew Sarah was looking into this. You knew she was working with me."

"Sarah was always going to be involved. Family is... complicated." The Machinist's voice softened. "I don't want to hurt her, Jack. I don't want to hurt anyone who doesn't deserve it. But I'm running out of time, and so are you. Marcus Pierce knows you're getting close. He's already making calls, already planning how to make you both disappear."

"Help me stop him. The legal way."

"The legal way?" The laugh again, bitter this time. "Where was the legal way for my father? For Jackie's father? For twenty-three men who died because a rich man wanted to save money on safety inspections? The law protects people like Harrison. It always has. Someone has to balance the scales."

"Not like this. Not by becoming what you hate."

"Perhaps." A pause. "Perhaps you're right. But it's too late for me, Jackie. It was too late the moment I watched them pull my father's body from the wreckage. The only question now is: what are you willing to sacrifice to stop me?"

The line went dead.

Jack lowered the phone slowly. Sarah was crying silently, tears streaming down her face.

"My cousin," she whispered. "David's son. His name is Daniel. I haven't seen him since the funeral. He was just a kid."

"He's not a kid anymore." Jack walked to the window, looking out at the empty forest. "He's spent fifteen years planning this. Training. Learning how to kill without leaving evidence."

"We have to stop him, Jack. Not just for the people he's killed—for him. He wasn't always like this. He was sweet. He loved his father so much."

"I know." Jack turned back to her. "And we will stop him. But first, we need to figure out what Dunn knew. What he was going to tell us."

They searched the cabin. In a floorboard under the bed, Jack found a metal box. Inside were documents—inspection reports from 2009, photos of the sabotaged methane detectors, and a handwritten statement from Walter Dunn admitting that he'd been paid fifty thousand dollars to falsify his safety certification.

The payee: Harrison Steel Corporation. The authorizing signature: William Harrison Sr.

"This is it," Sarah said, her voice trembling. "Proof of criminal negligence. Proof of bribery. We can use this to—"

Jack's phone rang again. Not a text this time. A call. He didn't recognize the number.

"Morrison."

A woman's voice, frantic and afraid. "Detective Morrison? This is Karen Miller. I'm Emily's teacher. There's been an incident at the school."

Jack felt the blood drain from his face. "Emily? Is she okay? What happened?"

"She's fine. Physically. But... someone left a message for her. A package. The principal thinks you should come right away."

Jack was already moving toward the door. "What kind of package?"

"A box. Inside was..." The teacher's voice broke. "A gear, Detective. A rusted metal gear. And a note. It said: 'Tell your father to stop digging, or next time I send something that bleeds.'"

The world tilted. Jack gripped the doorframe, his knuckles white.

"I'm in Chicago. I'm three hours away."

"The police are here. They're watching her. But Detective... he was inside the school. Security footage shows a man in a maintenance uniform delivering the package at 9:00 AM. No one questioned him. He just walked in, left it in her locker, and walked out."

Three hours ago. While Jack was driving to the Poconos, The Machinist was in Chicago, visiting his daughter.

He always knew. He was always watching.

"Tell Emily I love her," Jack said, his voice barely above a whisper. "Tell her I'm coming. And tell her... tell her I'm going to make this right."

He ended the call and turned to Sarah. She was already on her feet, keys in hand. "Chicago," she said. "I'll drive. You can sleep in the car." "Sarah, you don't have to—" "He's my cousin, Jack. My blood. If he's targeting children now, then I failed him fifteen years ago when I let him disappear. I'm helping you stop him. End of discussion." They drove through the night, Jack staring at his phone, willing it to ring with news that Emily was safe, that The Machinist had made his point and would back off. But the screen stayed dark. Somewhere in Pennsylvania, Sarah broke the silence. "He's not going to hurt her, Jack. I know him. Or I knew him. He has a code. He only hurts people he thinks deserve it." "He killed Dunn. He killed three others." "Adults. People connected to the explosion. Emily is innocent." "So were the twenty-three workers who died." Jack's voice was harsh, bitter. "Everyone's innocent until someone decides they're not." They drove on, two people bound together by loss and secrets, racing toward a confrontation that had been fifteen years in the making. Behind them, in a cabin in the mountains, the body of Walter Dunn grew cold, another witness silenced, another truth buried. But Jack had the documents now. He had proof of Harrison's crimes, proof of the cover-up, proof that twenty-three men had died not from accident but from greed. The question was: would any of it matter? Or would The Machinist kill them all before justice could be served? The phone buzzed. A text from an unknown number. "Drive safe, Jackie. Your daughter is beautiful. Just like her grandfather was brave. Choose your next move carefully. Some pieces, once taken, can never be returned." Jack closed his eyes and let the rage wash over him. The game had changed. The stakes had become personal. And he was done playing by the rules.
