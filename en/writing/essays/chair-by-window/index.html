The Chair by the Window

March 1, 2026 · Home · Silence

The same room, the same chair—yet every day feels slightly different. Morning light shifts across the floorboards, marking time in silent increments that only the patient observer notices.

I have sat in this chair for three years now. It was given to me by my grandmother, who received it from her mother before her. The wood is worn smooth in places where hands have rested, where bodies have settled into its curves with the familiarity of old friends.

By the window, I watch the seasons change. In spring, the light is green and uncertain, filtered through new leaves that tremble in the breeze. Summer brings a harsh brightness that forces me to draw the curtains by midday. Autumn gold fills the room with warmth that feels borrowed, temporary. And winter—winter offers a clarity, a stripped-down honesty that suits my mood.

Today, rain falls steadily against the glass. The world outside is soft, blurred at the edges. I sit with a cup of tea growing cold beside me, watching drops trace paths down the windowpane. Each follows its own logic, gathering speed, pausing, merging with others, continuing downward.

This chair has held my grief and my joy with equal patience. It does not distinguish between the weight of sorrow and the lightness of hope. It simply holds, as it was made to do, offering a place to be still in a world that rarely stops moving.
