The Night at the Station
Chapter 4

The Fog

The fog came down like a curtain, turning the world into something small and intimate. The mountains disappeared first, then the trees, then the distant lights of the village. Soon there was only the station, floating in a sea of gray.

He had always found fog unsettling. It reminded him of dreams—how you could never see far ahead, how the familiar became strange, how you might walk for hours and find yourself exactly where you started.

But tonight, something was different. The fog felt like protection. Like the world was giving him permission to be invisible, to let go of the person he had been trying so hard to become.

He took out the letter one more time. Not to read it, but to feel it. The paper was damp from the moisture in the air, soft as cloth. He thought about what it would mean to finally send it. To close that door. To admit that some words come too late to change anything.

The foghorn sounded in the distance—a low, mournful note that seemed to come from everywhere and nowhere. It was the sound of ships lost at sea, of travelers who couldn't find their way home.

He folded the letter carefully, placed it back in his pocket, and stepped to the edge of the platform. The tracks disappeared into the gray, leading nowhere, leading everywhere. He understood now why people disappear into fog. Sometimes vanishing is the only way to be found.
